## About Company
Free SAS is a French telecommunications company that provides internet, television, and mobile phone services.

## Incident and Exposure
On October 17, 2024, Free SAS experienced a data breach affecting 13.9 million records. The compromised data included names, physical addresses, phone numbers, genders, dates of birth, and for many records, International Bank Account Numbers (IBANs). This information was subsequently offered for sale and later made public.

The exposure of this data carries several risks. Your name, address, phone number, and date of birth can be used for identity theft or targeted phishing attacks. While Free stated that the exposed IBANs were "not enough to make a direct debit from a bank," this information, combined with other personal details, could still be exploited by fraudsters attempting to gain unauthorized access to financial accounts or impersonate you.

## Next Steps
You should immediately change passwords for any online accounts that used the same or similar credentials as your Free SAS account. Enable two-factor authentication (2FA) wherever possible to add an extra layer of security. Review your financial statements and credit reports regularly for any suspicious activity.

Be highly cautious of unsolicited communications, especially those claiming to be from Free SAS or other financial institutions. Phishing attempts may use the exposed personal information to appear more legitimate. Do not click on suspicious links or download attachments from unknown senders. Consider placing a fraud alert or credit freeze with credit bureaus to prevent new accounts from being opened in your name.

## GDPR Enforcement Record
The French Data Protection Authority (CNIL) issued two fines totaling €42 million on January 8, 2026, against Free SAS. These fines were for insufficient technical and organizational measures to ensure information security, directly relating to the company's failure to protect user data as required by GDPR Articles 5(1)(e) and 32. Earlier fines of €300,000 on December 8, 2022, and €300,000 on December 28, 2021, were also imposed by CNIL. These earlier fines were for insufficient fulfillment of data subjects' rights, specifically regarding requests for access, erasure, and objection to data processing, and are separate from the security failures that led to the 2024 data breach.
